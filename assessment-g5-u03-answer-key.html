Part 2 Source Task — Answer Key
Source Reminder
Bernal Díaz del Castillo, Spanish soldier, describing Tenochtitlan, c. 1519
"When we saw so many cities and villages built in the water… we were amazed. It was like the enchantments they tell of in books. And some of our soldiers even asked whether the things that we saw were not a dream."
A
Is this a primary or secondary source? Give TWO pieces of evidence.
✓ Suggested Answer

This is a primary source. Evidence:

1. The author says "when we saw" — he was present and personally witnessed the events.

2. The author uses first-person language ("our soldiers") indicating he was there at the time of the events described, not writing about them later.

Accept any two pieces of textual evidence showing the author was a firsthand witness. Deduct points if student says "secondary" or cannot cite specific text evidence.

B
One observation and one inference about the Aztec civilization.
✓ Suggested Answer

Strong observation: The source states that cities and villages were "built in the water" — the Aztec built their capital on an island in a lake, using causeways and canals.

Strong inference: The Aztec had advanced engineering and construction skills, because building a city of this size in a lake required sophisticated knowledge of water management, materials, and planning.

Award full credit for any observation cited directly from the text + a reasonable inference that goes beyond what the text directly states. Partial credit for an observation without an inference or vice versa.

C
Author's perspective and what might be left out.
✓ Suggested Answer

This author represents the perspective of a Spanish soldier/conqueror.

What it might leave out:

• The Aztec people's own perspective — how did they view the arrival of the Spanish?

• The violence and conquest happening at the same time — the Spanish came to conquer, not just admire.

• The full complexity of Aztec society — their government, religion, economy, and social structure are not captured in this brief reaction.

• The perspectives of peoples conquered by the Aztec who may have welcomed Spanish arrival.

Award full credit for identifying the author as a Spanish conqueror AND naming at least one meaningful gap in the source's perspective. Partial credit for identifying the author's perspective without naming what's missing.

Part 3 Civilizations Comparison — Scoring Guide
Choose TWO civilizations from Unit 3. Make a claim about the most important way they were similar OR different. Use specific evidence from each and explain your reasoning.
Scoring Notes — Civilizations Comparison Claim
Secure (10–12 pts)
Student names two specific civilizations and makes a clear, arguable claim about their similarity or difference (not just "they were different"). Uses at least one specific piece of evidence per civilization (e.g., Aztec = island city with 200,000+ people; Inca = mountain road network spanning 2,500 miles). Reasoning explains WHY this comparison matters — what it reveals about how geography shaped these peoples. Example strong claim: "The Aztec and Inca civilizations both showed remarkable engineering, but their environments led them to different solutions — the Aztec mastered water while the Inca mastered mountains."
Developing (6–9 pts)
Student names two civilizations and makes a comparison but uses general evidence. May list facts without connecting to a clear argument. "The Maya had pyramids and the Inca had roads" — accurate but not yet a claim about what this means.
Beginning (4–5 pts)
Student names civilizations but comparison is very general ("they were both old" or "they both had cities"). Little or no specific evidence used. No clear claim distinguishable from a list of facts.

Strong pairings to look for: Aztec vs. Inca (island city vs. mountain empire); Maya vs. Mound Builders (both large cities but different environments); Ancestral Pueblo vs. Great Plains indigenous peoples (sedentary vs. nomadic). Any two civilizations with specific evidence earns full credit.

Part 4 Virtue Lens — Scoring Guide
Courtesy: What does courtesy look like when studying ancient civilizations? Give one specific example of how a courteous historian would approach this topic differently from someone careless or disrespectful.
Scoring Notes — Virtue Lens Reflection
Secure (3 pts)
Student evaluates why courtesy matters — not just defines it. Example of secure response: "A courteous historian uses 'Ancestral Pueblo' instead of 'Anasazi' because descendants of that culture still live today and have expressed preference for the respectful name. Using the wrong name shows disrespect for living communities." OR: "A courteous historian would not describe the Aztec or Maya as 'primitive' because these civilizations had mathematics, astronomy, and engineering equal to or more advanced than European civilizations at the same time." Connects courtesy to accuracy AND respect for living descendants.
Developing (2 pts)
Student says a courteous historian uses correct names or doesn't use insulting language, but doesn't explain why this matters for accuracy or for living descendants.
Beginning (1 pt)
Student defines courtesy generally ("being nice") without connecting it to specific practices in historical study.
