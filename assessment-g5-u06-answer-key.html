Part 2 Source Task — Answer Key
Adapted from the journal of Christopher Columbus, October 1492, describing his first encounter with the Taíno people of the Caribbean
"They are well-built people of handsome stature. They bear no arms, nor know thereof. They are very gentle and do not know what evil is. I believe that they would become Christians very easily, for it seems to me that they had no religion. With fifty men we could subjugate them all and make them do whatever we wish."
A
Two things Columbus says about the Taíno, with text evidence.
Suggested Answer

1. Columbus says the Taíno "bear no arms, nor know thereof" — they had no weapons and did not know how to fight.

2. Columbus says they "do not know what evil is" and are "very gentle" — describing them as innocent or naive.

Accept any two direct quotations with accurate interpretation. Deduct if student only paraphrases without quoting from the source.

B
Problem with Columbus's claim about Taíno religion and what it tells us about his perspective.
Suggested Answer

The Taíno did have a religion — they practiced a spiritual tradition that included beliefs in nature spirits called zemís. Columbus could not understand or recognize it because it was different from his own Christian faith. His perspective was limited by his own cultural assumptions: if something was not Christianity, he did not count it as a religion. This is called ethnocentrism — judging another culture by your own standards.

Award full credit for identifying that Columbus misunderstood or dismissed Taíno religion due to cultural bias. Partial credit for noting it was inaccurate without explaining why.

C
Columbus's real motives revealed at the end of the source, and how this changes interpretation of his earlier comments.
Suggested Answer

When Columbus writes "with fifty men we could subjugate them all," his real motive becomes clear: conquest and control. He was not just exploring — he was assessing how easy the Taíno would be to dominate. This reframes his earlier positive comments: calling them "gentle" and "unarmed" was not admiration — it was a strategic assessment of their vulnerability. A historian reading this source must recognize that the seemingly kind observations are actually part of the logic of conquest.

Award full credit for identifying conquest/control as the real motive AND explaining how this reframes the earlier positive descriptions. Partial credit for noting the motive without connecting it to earlier comments.

Part 3 Explorer Impact Claim — Scoring Guide
Write a more complete and honest account of the impact of European exploration, including both positive and negative consequences, with a claim about the most important thing we must understand.
Scoring Notes
Secure (10–12 pts)
Student makes a nuanced claim acknowledging complexity — neither "exploration was all good" nor "all bad." Includes specific positive consequences (new trade routes, new crops like corn and potatoes reaching Europe, geographic knowledge) AND specific negative consequences (destruction of indigenous civilizations, Columbian Exchange diseases killing 50–90% of indigenous populations in some regions, beginning of the Atlantic slave trade, forced labor). Reasoning explains what the student believes is the most important truth to understand and why it matters.
Developing (6–9 pts)
Student acknowledges both sides but one is much more developed than the other. May include specific facts for one side but vague claims for the other. Claim is present but not fully supported.
Beginning (4–5 pts)
Student presents only one side, or presents both sides with no specific evidence. No clear claim about what is most important to understand.
Part 4 Virtue Lens — Scoring Guide
Courage + Responsibility: In what way does studying Unit 6 require courage as a learner? Give one specific difficult truth and explain why it matters to include it.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3 pts)
Student names a specific difficult truth from the unit (e.g., Columbus's journal revealing conquest motives; disease killing millions of indigenous people; enslaved Africans brought to the Americas through the Columbian Exchange) AND explains why it matters — not just that it is sad, but why historians must include it to give an accurate account. Strong example: "A difficult truth is that the Columbian Exchange brought diseases that killed up to 90% of indigenous peoples in some regions. It matters to include this because without it, 'exploration' sounds only like adventure and discovery — when really it was also catastrophic for millions of people."
Developing (2 pts)
Student names a difficult truth but the explanation of WHY it matters is general rather than specific to the historical impact.
Beginning (1 pt)
Student says something about courage without connecting it to a specific difficult truth from the unit.
