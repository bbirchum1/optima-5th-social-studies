Part 2 Source Task
Primary Source · Oral Tradition, Written Record
Adapted from the Great Law of Peace (Gayanashagowa) of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy — oral tradition of the Seneca, Cayuga, Onondaga, Oneida, and Mohawk Nations, c. 1450–1600
"We, the people, to form a union, to establish peace and good will, to be called the Five Nations Confederacy, shall roll into one bundle all of our strength and present it to one another. The council fire shall burn at the center, and all nations shall gather around it as equals to make decisions together."

Read the source carefully, then answer each question using evidence from the text.

A
What did the Haudenosaunee Confederacy create? Use at least two specific details from the source to describe what they were building together.
B
This source describes a government where nations gathered "as equals to make decisions together." What does this tell you about Haudenosaunee values? Write one inference about what the confederacy cared about.
C
This source describes the Haudenosaunee forming a union of five nations. How does this compare to what you know about how the United States government was later formed? Describe one similarity.
Part 3 Regions Comparison Claim

The Hasty Herald claims: "Native Americans all lived the same way — hunting animals and moving around. Geography had nothing to do with how they lived." Choose TWO of the four Native American regions you studied in Unit 4. Make a claim that explains how geography shaped the culture of each region differently. Use specific evidence from both regions to support your claim, and explain your reasoning. Do not just list facts — connect the evidence to your argument.

Aim for at least 4 sentences: claim → evidence from each region → reasoning

Part 4 Virtue Lens Reflection
Courtesy

Courtesy means treating people with respect — including the peoples you study in history. Native American communities are still alive today, and their descendants live in every region of the United States. When historians study indigenous peoples, courtesy looks like: using the names communities prefer for themselves, not calling cultures "primitive," presenting full and accurate stories rather than stereotypes, and recognizing that these are not just "ancient history" but living traditions. Think about your study of Unit 4. What is one specific way a courteous historian would study Native American regional cultures differently from someone who is careless or disrespectful? Give a clear, specific example.
