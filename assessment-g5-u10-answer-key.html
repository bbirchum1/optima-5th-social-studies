Part 1 Vocabulary in the Field — Answer Key
Q1 — Fill in the Blank
campaign — A military campaign is a series of operations toward a specific goal. Washington's New Jersey campaigns (Trenton, Princeton) are the key examples from the unit.
Q2 — Fill in the Blank
alliance — France became America's most important foreign ally after Saratoga (1777), providing troops, naval support, and financing.
Q3 — Fill in the Blank
Articles of Confederation — Ratified 1781, America's first governing document. Key weakness: no power to tax, no strong executive, each state kept most power.
Q4 — Fill in the Blank
Constitutional Convention — Met in Philadelphia, May–September 1787. Produced the U.S. Constitution. Delegates included Madison, Hamilton, Washington (presiding), Franklin.
Q5 — Fill in the Blank
Northwest Ordinance — 1787. Created the process for admitting new states (3 to 5 from the Northwest Territory). First federal legislation to limit slavery's expansion.

Part 2 Primary Source Analysis — Answer Key & Guidance
Q2A — Valley Forge Conditions (Two Specific Details)

Students should cite directly or paraphrase from the source. Accept any two of the following:

  • Soldiers had no clothing to cover themselves ("without clothes to cover their nakedness")
  • Soldiers had no blankets to sleep on ("without blankets to lie on")
  • Soldiers had no shoes ("without shoes")
  • Despite these conditions, they endured without complaint ("submitting to it without a murmur")

Award full credit for any two details that are specific and drawn from the text (not general background knowledge about Valley Forge).

Q2B — Washington's Values and the Virtue He Describes

Washington valued perseverance and self-discipline. He used the words "patience and obedience" to describe soldiers who chose to continue serving despite unbearable suffering — without complaint, without deserting.

The virtues on display: perseverance (continuing without giving up) and courage (staying in the face of terrible hardship). Accept either or both. Strong answers will note that Washington seems almost in awe of his men — his admiration tells us what kind of leadership and character he valued most.

Q2C — Missing Perspectives

Washington's letter is a leader's view — shaped by his role and his goal of praising the men. Missing perspectives include:

  • The soldiers themselves — many wrote diaries and letters that describe despair, near-mutiny, and desertion that Washington's letter glosses over. His account says "without a murmur" — soldier diaries often tell a different story.
  • Enslaved laborers who served with or near the Continental Army — their experiences are almost entirely absent from the official record.
  • Women camp followers (wives, nurses, cooks) who sustained the army at Valley Forge and whose work was rarely acknowledged in official documents.
  • Loyalists living near Valley Forge whose property was seized and whose perspective on the Continental Army was deeply hostile.

Award credit for any thoughtful, specific answer that identifies a group whose experience is not captured in Washington's account and explains why their perspective might differ.

Part 3 Hasty Herald — Extended Response Scoring Guidance
Model Factors — Accept any well-supported combination

The Hasty Herald's claim ("just bravery and patriotism") ignores the complex web of factors that actually decided the war. Strong responses will identify at least three from the following categories:

  • French Alliance (Diplomatic): After Saratoga (1777), France officially allied with America — providing troops, the French navy (critical at Yorktown, 1781), and massive loans. Without French naval support at Yorktown, Cornwallis could not have been trapped.
  • Military Training at Valley Forge: Baron von Steuben turned a ragged, undisciplined force into a professional army during the winter of 1777–78. The army that emerged from Valley Forge was fundamentally different from the one that entered.
  • British Supply and Logistics Problems: Britain fought 3,000+ miles from home. Supply lines across the Atlantic were slow, expensive, and vulnerable. The longer the war lasted, the harder it became for Britain to sustain.
  • American Geographic Advantage: Americans knew the terrain; British generals did not. Guerrilla tactics (hit-and-run, Swamp Fox), use of rivers and forests, and the sheer size of the continent made British conquest extremely difficult.
  • Diplomacy — Spain and the Netherlands: Spain and the Netherlands also entered the war against Britain, stretching British military resources across multiple theaters.
  • British Public Opinion: The war became increasingly unpopular in Britain; Parliament debated ending it; the cost in lives and money mounted without decisive results.
Scoring Rubric — Part 3
LevelDescription
Secure Names at least THREE specific factors from different categories (military, diplomatic, geographic, or logistical), each supported with specific evidence. Makes a clear claim at the beginning. Goes substantially beyond "they were brave."
Developing Names TWO specific factors with some evidence. May include bravery/patriotism as one factor but identifies at least one concrete, specific additional factor (e.g., French alliance). Response has a claim but may be brief or underdeveloped on evidence.
Beginning Repeats "they were brave" or "they believed in freedom" without naming specific military, diplomatic, geographic, or economic factors. No evidence. May list factors without explanation.
Part 4 Virtue Reflection — Scoring Guidance (Perseverance)
What to Look For

This is a personal reflection — there is no single correct answer. Score based on the quality of connection and the specificity of the personal example.

Strong responses will:

  • Identify a specific moment from the student's own learning this year (not a general statement like "school was hard sometimes")
  • Describe what made that moment require perseverance — the struggle, the temptation to give up, the choice to continue
  • Make a genuine connection to Valley Forge — same virtue, different scale: soldiers chose to stay through suffering; the student chose to keep working through difficulty
Scoring Rubric — Part 4
LevelDescription
Secure Gives a specific, concrete personal moment of perseverance — identifies what was hard, what the temptation was (to quit, to skip, to give up), and what it took to continue. Makes a meaningful connection to Valley Forge — not just "they persevered too" but something that captures the parallel (staying when it's hard, training/working when you'd rather stop, etc.).
Developing Makes a personal connection but it's general ("I had a hard lesson once and I kept going"). Mentions Valley Forge but the connection is surface-level ("they also had to persevere").
Beginning Describes Valley Forge without a personal reflection, OR describes a personal moment without any connection to Valley Forge or the virtue of perseverance.
