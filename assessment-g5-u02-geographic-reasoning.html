Part 2 Map Source Task
Primary Source · Historical Map Description
Description of a colonial-era map of North America, c. 1755 — from a British cartographer's notes
"The major settlements along the eastern coast of North America lie between approximately 30 and 45 degrees north latitude. The Appalachian Mountains appear as a long barrier running north to south, cutting off easy movement west. The coastal plains between the mountains and the sea are where most farms and towns appear. Rivers running east to west — the Potomac, the James, the Connecticut — seem to serve as roads into the interior."

Read the source carefully, then answer each question using evidence from the text.

A
What does this source tell us about WHERE early colonists settled? Give TWO specific details from the source as evidence.
B
Using what you know about latitude and climate from Unit 2, explain WHY colonists settled in the 30–45° north latitude band. This is an inference — the source doesn't tell you directly.
C
What important information does this source NOT show? What groups of people and perspectives are missing from a British cartographer's map of North America in 1755?
Part 3 Geographic Reasoning Claim

Ms. Avery always says: "Geography is not just the setting for history — it IS history." Using at least TWO geographic tools from Unit 2 (such as latitude and longitude, physical features, climate, or regions), explain how geography helped determine where people settled and why. Make a clear claim, support it with specific evidence from the lessons, and explain your reasoning.

Aim for at least 4 sentences: claim → evidence → reasoning → conclusion

Part 4 Virtue Lens Reflection
Perseverance

Mapmakers in the 1700s sometimes spent years completing a single map of North America — measuring, correcting mistakes, and starting over when new information arrived. Perseverance means continuing carefully even when the work is hard and takes a long time. What does perseverance look like in geographic or historical work? Give one specific example of why a geographer, mapmaker, or historian would need to practice perseverance to get the story right.
