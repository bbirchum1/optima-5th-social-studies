Part 2 Source Task — Answer Key
Source Reminder
Great Law of Peace (Gayanashagowa), Haudenosaunee Confederacy, c. 1450–1600
"We, the people, to form a union, to establish peace and good will, to be called the Five Nations Confederacy, shall roll into one bundle all of our strength and present it to one another. The council fire shall burn at the center, and all nations shall gather around it as equals to make decisions together."
A
What did the Haudenosaunee create? Two specific details.
✓ Suggested Answer

The Haudenosaunee created a confederacy (union) of five nations to establish peace.

Detail 1: The source says they would "roll into one bundle all of our strength" — meaning all five nations combined their power together.

Detail 2: The source says "all nations shall gather around [the council fire] as equals to make decisions together" — meaning they formed a shared government where every nation had equal say.

Accept any two textual details. Strong answers quote directly from the source.

B
What does this tell you about Haudenosaunee values? One inference.
✓ Suggested Answer

Strong inference: The Haudenosaunee valued equality and shared decision-making. The fact that "all nations gather as equals" shows they believed no single nation should hold all the power — decisions should be made together.

Also accept: They valued peace over war (source explicitly names establishing peace as the goal). They valued unity and cooperation. Award full credit for any reasonable inference supported by the text.

C
One similarity to how the United States government was later formed.
✓ Suggested Answer

Strong similarity: Both the Haudenosaunee Confederacy and the United States joined separate, independent groups together into a shared union while allowing each part to keep some of its own identity. Just as the five nations kept their identities while joining the confederacy, the thirteen states kept their identities while joining the U.S. under the Constitution.

Also accept: Both had a council/congress where representatives made decisions together. Both were formed to establish peace and resolve conflicts between groups. Award full credit for any accurate parallel. Students haven't studied the Constitution yet (that's Unit 11), so accept general knowledge here.

Part 3 Regions Comparison — Scoring Guide
Choose TWO of the four Native American regions. Make a claim explaining how geography shaped each region's culture differently. Use specific evidence from both regions.
Scoring Notes — Regions Comparison Claim
Secure (10–12 pts)
Student names two specific regions and makes a clear claim connecting geography to culture (not just "they were different"). Uses specific evidence from each region tied to geography: e.g., Great Plains = flat grasslands with no trees → buffalo were the main resource → tipis were portable because they followed herds; Pacific Northwest = forests, rivers, ocean → cedar for longhouses and canoes, salmon for food → potlatch to share surplus wealth. Reasoning explains WHY geography produced those cultural choices.
Developing (6–9 pts)
Student names two regions and describes cultural practices accurately but doesn't fully connect them to geography. "Great Plains tribes used tipis and Pacific Northwest tribes had potlatches" — accurate but the geographic reasoning is missing or weak.
Beginning (4–5 pts)
Student names regions but descriptions are vague or inaccurate. Little or no specific cultural evidence. No connection made between geography and culture.

Strong pairings: Great Plains vs. Pacific Northwest (nomadic vs. sedentary, very different resources); Desert Southwest vs. Eastern Woodland (farming pueblo peoples vs. confederacy-forming woodland tribes); any pairing works if geography is central to the argument.

Part 4 Virtue Lens — Scoring Guide
Courtesy: What is one specific way a courteous historian would study Native American regional cultures differently from someone careless or disrespectful?
Scoring Notes — Virtue Lens Reflection
Secure (3 pts)
Student gives a specific, concrete example that shows they understand courtesy as both respectful AND accurate. Strong examples: "A courteous historian uses 'Haudenosaunee' instead of 'Iroquois' because the Haudenosaunee people use that name for themselves, and using the name they prefer shows respect for their identity." OR "A courteous historian would not say Great Plains tribes were 'uncivilized' just because they didn't build cities — tipi design was sophisticated engineering that showed deep knowledge of materials and weather." Connects courtesy to accuracy, not just politeness.
Developing (2 pts)
Student says a courteous historian uses respectful language or avoids stereotypes, but doesn't give a specific example tied to the unit content.
Beginning (1 pt)
Student defines courtesy generally ("being nice to people") without connecting it to the study of Native American history.
