Scoring Overview — G5-U02
Part 1
6 items
Part 2
3 questions
Parts 3–4
12 pts rubric

Parts 1–2: score on accuracy (each item worth 1–2 points at teacher's discretion). Parts 3–4: use the 4-criterion rubric (Beginning=1, Developing=2, Secure=3, max 12 points). Suggested total: 24 points.

Part 1 Knowledge Check — Answer Key
1
Lines of __________ run east-west around the globe and measure how far north or south a place is from the equator.
✓ Correct Answer

latitude

2
Lines of __________ run north-south and measure how far east or west a place is from the __________.
✓ Correct Answer

longitude ; Prime Meridian

Accept either order in student response as long as both terms are present.

3
Mountains, rivers, valleys, and plains are examples of __________ __________ — the natural landforms that shaped where people settled and how they traveled.
✓ Correct Answer

physical features

4
The long-term weather patterns of a place — whether it is usually hot, cold, rainy, or dry — is called its __________.
✓ Correct Answer

climate

5
A large area that shares geography, culture, or economy — like the Great Plains or the Southeast — is called a __________.
✓ Correct Answer

region

6
Matching: Territory / Equator / Coordinates / Prime Meridian
✓ Correct Answers

Territory = C — An area the U.S. governs but that has not yet become a state

Equator = A — The line of 0° latitude that divides the globe into Northern and Southern Hemispheres

Coordinates = D — A pair of latitude and longitude numbers that pinpoint an exact location

Prime Meridian = B — The line of 0° longitude

Part 2 Map Source Task — Answer Key
Source Reminder
Description of a colonial-era map of North America, c. 1755 — British cartographer's notes
"The major settlements along the eastern coast of North America lie between approximately 30 and 45 degrees north latitude. The Appalachian Mountains appear as a long barrier running north to south, cutting off easy movement west. The coastal plains between the mountains and the sea are where most farms and towns appear. Rivers running east to west — the Potomac, the James, the Connecticut — seem to serve as roads into the interior."
A
What does this source tell us about WHERE early colonists settled? Give TWO specific details from the source.
✓ Suggested Answer

Strong responses include TWO of these details:

1. Settlements were concentrated between 30–45° north latitude along the eastern coast.

2. Most farms and towns appeared on coastal plains between the Appalachians and the sea.

3. The Appalachian Mountains acted as a barrier that blocked easy movement west.

4. Rivers like the Potomac, James, and Connecticut served as pathways into the interior.

Accept any two details pulled directly from the source text. Deduct points if student only paraphrases without citing specific evidence.

B
Using what you know about latitude and climate, explain WHY colonists settled in the 30–45° north latitude band. (Inference)
✓ Suggested Answer

Strong responses explain: The 30–45° north latitude band has a moderate climate — not too hot, not too cold — which made it suitable for farming and year-round living. This latitude range receives enough rainfall and has long enough growing seasons for crops like tobacco, corn, and wheat. Places near the equator (lower latitudes) can be too hot and humid; places farther north (higher latitudes) can be too cold for reliable agriculture.

Give full credit for any response that connects latitude to climate to human settlement choice. A response that just says "it was a good climate" without explaining the latitude connection earns partial credit.

C
What important information does this source NOT show? What groups of people and perspectives are missing?
✓ Suggested Answer

Strong responses identify at least ONE of these missing perspectives:

• Native American nations who already lived on this land and whose settlements, trade routes, and territorial boundaries are not shown.

• Enslaved Africans who were also present in these colonies but whose lives and movements are invisible on a cartographer's map.

• French and Spanish settlements in other parts of North America that a British mapmaker might downplay or omit.

• The perspective of the land itself — the map shows geography from a European colonial point of view.

Accept any thoughtful response that identifies a missing group and explains why their absence matters.

Part 3 Geographic Reasoning Claim — Scoring Guide
Ms. Avery: "Geography is not just the setting for history — it IS history." Using at least TWO geographic tools from Unit 2, explain how geography helped determine where people settled and why.
Scoring Notes — Geographic Reasoning Claim
Secure (10–12 pts)
Student makes a clear, specific claim (e.g., "Geography directly determined where colonists settled because latitude controlled climate and mountains blocked westward movement"). Uses TWO or more geographic tools with specific evidence (e.g., cites the 30–45° latitude band and the Appalachian barrier). Explains the reasoning — how and why geography mattered, not just that it did. Shows awareness of consequence (who was helped or hurt by geographic location).
Developing (6–9 pts)
Student makes a claim and uses one or two geographic tools as evidence, but reasoning is general ("geography affected settlement") rather than specific. May name latitude and climate but not explain the connection clearly. May not address consequences.
Beginning (4–5 pts)
Student mentions geography and settlement but does not make a clear claim or use specific geographic tools as evidence. Response may list facts without connecting them to the central argument.

Strong evidence to look for: latitude and climate connection; Appalachians as barrier; rivers as highways; coastal plains as farmable land; the 30–45° band and its growing season.

Part 4 Virtue Lens — Scoring Guide
Perseverance: Mapmakers in the 1700s sometimes spent years completing a single map. What does perseverance look like in geographic or historical work? Give one specific example.
Scoring Notes — Virtue Lens Reflection
Secure (3 pts)
Student evaluates how perseverance shaped geographic or historical work — not just names the virtue. Example: "A mapmaker who sailed back and corrected a coastline error showed perseverance because they valued accuracy over convenience. Without perseverance, maps would be full of errors that led future explorers astray." Connects the virtue to a consequence.
Developing (2 pts)
Student connects perseverance to geographic or historical work with a general example. Mentions difficulty of the work and continuing anyway, but doesn't explain the consequence or significance.
Beginning (1 pt)
Student defines perseverance or names it in context of geography but does not give a meaningful example or explain why it matters for the work.
Rubric Full Scoring Rubric — Parts 3 & 4
Criterion Beginning (1 pt) Developing (2 pts) Secure (3 pts)
Content Knowledge Basic geographic facts stated; misses connections between geography and settlement Explains geographic tools or features accurately with some context Accuracy + geographic context + explains how geography shaped historical decisions
Evidence & Corroboration Weak or vague evidence; names a feature without explaining it Uses relevant geographic evidence from the lessons Strong evidence from two or more geographic tools; corroborates with map and text
Historical/Civic Argument Claim stated with little or no reasoning Claim + supporting geographic evidence present Claim + evidence + reasoning + awareness of how geography created consequences
Virtue & Civic Reasoning Names perseverance or responsibility with minimal connection to geographic work Connects virtue to a specific geographic or historical task Evaluates how perseverance or responsibility shaped geographic or historical choices
Total Possible: 12 points (3 pts × 4 criteria)
