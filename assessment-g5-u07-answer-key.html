Part 2 Source Task — Answer Key
Adapted from the memoir of Olaudah Equiano, formerly enslaved, c. 1789 — describing his experience aboard a slave ship during the Middle Passage
"I was soon put down under the decks, and there I received such a salutation in my nostrils as I had never experienced in my life: so that, with the loathsomeness of the stench, and crying together, I became so sick and low that I was not able to eat, nor had I the least desire to taste anything. I now wished for the last friend, death, to relieve me."
A
Two specific details about the Middle Passage from the text.
Suggested Answer

1. The conditions below decks were horrifying — Equiano describes "the loathsomeness of the stench" and people crying together, showing that enslaved Africans were packed below deck in unbearable, filthy conditions.

2. He became so ill he could not eat and "wished for the last friend, death" — showing that the physical suffering and psychological despair of the Middle Passage were so extreme that death seemed preferable to continuing.

Accept any two details directly supported by the text. Award full credit for quoting and interpreting; partial credit for paraphrasing without quoting.

B
Does writing a memoir later make it less reliable as historical evidence?
Suggested Answer

Writing the memoir later does not necessarily make it less reliable. Equiano was describing his own lived experience — something as traumatic as the Middle Passage would be remembered vividly for a lifetime. Memoirs written after the fact are considered primary sources for the person's perspective even if minor details might vary over time. The emotional truth of the experience — the conditions, the suffering, the despair — has high reliability as a firsthand account of what enslaved Africans endured.

Accept: "It may mean some specific details aren't perfectly accurate, but the emotional and experiential truth is highly reliable." Also accept well-reasoned arguments that it is somewhat less reliable, as long as the student acknowledges it is still valuable evidence. Deduct if student simply says "yes, it's less reliable" without reasoning.

C
Whose perspective is missing from most colonial records, and why does Equiano's memoir matter?
Suggested Answer

The perspectives of enslaved Africans are almost entirely absent from most colonial-era documents. The vast majority of records were written by white European colonists, plantation owners, merchants, and government officials — people with power. Enslaved people were not permitted to read or write in most colonies, so firsthand accounts of enslavement are extremely rare. Equiano's memoir is one of the only surviving firsthand accounts of the slave trade and enslavement from an African perspective, making it invaluable for historians trying to understand the full reality of colonial life — not just the experience of those in power, but of those who were enslaved.

Award full credit for identifying enslaved Africans as the missing perspective AND explaining why Equiano's source is rare and important. Partial credit for identifying the missing group without explaining why the source is significant.

Part 3 Colonial Life Claim — Scoring Guide
Compare the experience of TWO different groups in colonial America. Show how colonial life was NOT equal opportunity for everyone. Use specific evidence.
Scoring Notes
Secure (10–12 pts)
Student clearly compares two specific groups (e.g., enslaved Africans vs. wealthy plantation owners; Puritan settlers vs. indentured servants; New England colonists vs. Southern enslaved people) with specific evidence for each. Shows that colonial life was profoundly unequal — not just "different." Strong responses will note: enslaved Africans had no freedom, no wages, no legal rights, and were considered property; wealthy planters owned land, held political power, served in colonial assemblies; indentured servants eventually gained freedom while enslaved people typically did not. Makes a clear claim about what this inequality means historically.
Developing (6–9 pts)
Student identifies two groups and notes that their experiences were different, but evidence for one group is vague or underdeveloped. May say "enslaved people had a hard life" without specifics. Claim is present but lacks full support.
Beginning (4–5 pts)
Student describes only one group, or lists facts about colonial life without comparing or making a claim about inequality. No specific evidence connecting to the experiences of distinct groups.
Part 4 Virtue Lens — Scoring Guide
Service: Give one specific example of how a historian serves justice by including a difficult or overlooked perspective. Explain why that act of inclusion matters.
Scoring Notes
Secure (3 pts)
Student gives a specific example of a historian serving justice through inclusion of an overlooked perspective (e.g., including Equiano's memoir in the historical record means future students learn about the slave trade from an enslaved person's viewpoint; documenting the experiences of indentured servants gives a fuller picture than only reading about wealthy colonists; preserving Native American oral histories alongside European colonial accounts). AND explains the impact: why does including this perspective matter? Strong example: "A historian serves justice by including Equiano's memoir in the historical record. Without it, students would only read accounts written by Europeans, which described the slave trade in terms of economics and trade routes — not in terms of the suffering of real people. Including Equiano's account makes the reality of enslavement visible and ensures that the people who were most affected are not erased from history."
Developing (2 pts)
Student mentions service or including overlooked voices without giving a specific example or connecting it clearly to the impact on the historical record.
Beginning (1 pt)
Student defines service in general terms without connecting it to historical study or a specific example from the unit.
