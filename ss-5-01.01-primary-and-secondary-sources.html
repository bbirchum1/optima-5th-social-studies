★ Editor's Lens · Honesty ★
An honest editor checks the source before printing the claim. It's tempting to repeat what other papers say, especially when a story sounds exciting. But honest journalism — like honest history — means slowing down to ask, "Where did this come from? Who said it, and when? Can I trust it?"

📚 Two Kinds of Sources

Historians and newspaper editors work with two main kinds of sources. Both are useful — but they are not the same, and an honest editor learns to tell them apart.

Kind 1
Primary Source
A source created at the time of the event, by someone who was actually there. It's a piece of the original record.
Examples A soldier's letter home from a war. A diary kept by a colonist. A photograph from Ellis Island in 1905. The original Declaration of Independence. An oral history recorded by a member of the Lakota Nation.
Kind 2
Secondary Source
A source created later, by someone who studied the event using primary sources. It tells you about the event.
Examples A textbook chapter about the Civil War. A biography of Frederick Douglass written in 2010. A Wikipedia article about Ellis Island. A documentary film about the Declaration of Independence.

Here's the part the Hasty Herald got wrong: primary sources don't tell the whole truth by themselves either. A soldier's letter only tells you what one soldier saw. A diary only tells you what one person thought. Honest editors gather many sources — primary AND secondary — and ask careful questions before printing anything.

★ Fact-Check Room ★
Before the Gazette Goes to Print
Check each claim carefully. An honest paper publishes only what holds up.
1 A photograph taken at Ellis Island in 1905 would be classified as which kind of source?
A A secondary source, because it shows old buildings.
B A primary source, because it was created at the time of the event by someone who was there.
C Neither — photographs cannot be historical sources.
2 A 2018 textbook chapter that describes how the Declaration of Independence was written is best described as:
A A primary source, because it talks about the Declaration.
B Both primary and secondary, because it is a real book.
C A secondary source, because it was written later by someone studying primary sources.
3 An honest editor at The Republic Gazette would respond to a single primary source by:
A Printing it word-for-word as the whole truth, because it's from the event itself.
B Treating it as important evidence — but also looking for other sources and perspectives before publishing a claim.
C Ignoring it, because primary sources are usually too old to trust.
