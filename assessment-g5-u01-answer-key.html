Part 1 Knowledge Check — Answer Key
1
A soldier's letter written during the Civil War is an example of a __________ because it was created at the time by someone who was there.
✓ Suggested Answer
primary source
2
A 2018 textbook chapter about the Declaration of Independence is an example of a __________ because it was written later using research.
✓ Suggested Answer
secondary source
3
A historian uses a __________ to show events in order, grouped into named stretches of time called __________.
✓ Suggested Answer
timeline  /  historical period
4
The three parts of a strong historical claim are __________ (what you think is true), __________ (specific facts that support it), and __________ (explaining why the evidence proves the claim).
✓ Suggested Answer
claim  /  evidence  /  reasoning
5
A __________ is a direction tool on a map that helps historians understand which way people traveled and why.
✓ Suggested Answer
compass rose
6
Matching — A) Map   B) Globe   C) Compass Rose   D) Timeline

___ Shows the shape of continents as they really curve on Earth
___ Shows events in the order they happened
___ Shows routes, resources, and boundaries flat
___ Shows direction of travel
✓ Suggested Answers
Globe = B  (Shows continents curved on Earth)
Timeline = D  (Shows events in order)
Map = A  (Shows routes, resources, boundaries flat)
Compass Rose = C  (Shows direction of travel)
Part 2 Source Task — Answer Key
Primary Source · Letter
Excerpt from a letter written by a Continental soldier at Valley Forge, December 1777
"We have been without proper clothing for weeks. Last night we had nothing to eat but firecake and water. Still, we remain. Our cause is just."
A
Is this a primary source or a secondary source? Give TWO pieces of evidence from the source itself to support your answer.
✓ Suggested Answer

Primary source.

Evidence 1: The source was written in December 1777 — during the event itself. A primary source is created at the time by someone present.

Evidence 2: The author uses first-person language ("We have been," "we remain") — the writer was personally present at Valley Forge, not reporting from a distance later.

B
What does this source tell us about conditions at Valley Forge? Write one specific detail you can observe and one inference you can make.
✓ Suggested Answer

Observable detail: Soldiers lacked food and clothing at Valley Forge — the source states they had been "without proper clothing for weeks" and had "nothing to eat but firecake and water."

Inference: Despite terrible conditions, soldiers believed the cause was worth staying for. The phrase "our cause is just" shows they chose to remain even while suffering greatly — this suggests strong commitment or morale despite the hardship.

C
What important information might this source NOT show? Whose perspective is missing?
✓ Suggested Answer

Missing perspectives include:

• The British perspective on the war and Valley Forge
• The perspective of officers vs. common soldiers (this is one enlisted soldier's view)
• The experiences of enslaved people who served in or were affected by the war
• Civilian perspectives from nearby communities who also suffered
• The broader military situation — this single letter cannot show how the whole army felt

Part 3 Historian's Claim — Scoring Notes
Original prompt: "Ms. Avery always says: 'An honest historian never relies on just one source.' Using what you learned in Unit 1, explain why historians need more than one kind of source to tell a complete story. Make a clear claim, use evidence from the lessons, and explain your reasoning."
Scoring Guidance by Level
Secure — 3 pts
Names BOTH primary and secondary sources by type. Gives specific examples from the lesson (e.g., "a soldier's letter from Valley Forge" and "a textbook chapter about the Declaration"). Explains WHY each adds something the other can't — e.g., "a soldier's letter shows personal experience and emotion, but doesn't show the big picture — a secondary source uses many letters and records to explain the whole war." Reasoning is explicit and tied back to the claim. May address the counter-perspective (one source might seem enough, but…).
Developing — 2 pts
Names two source types and gives examples, but reasoning is thin or implicit. Does not fully explain the causal connection between source type and completeness of the story. May write "you need both kinds" without explaining what each one contributes that the other cannot.
Beginning — 1 pt
Lists or defines source types without making an argument for why both are needed. May correctly identify primary and secondary sources without connecting them to the idea of a "complete story." Little or no claim structure (claim, evidence, reasoning).
Part 4 Virtue Lens — Honesty — Scoring Notes
Original prompt: "Honesty means telling the truth, even when it's hard. What does honesty look like in the work of a historian? Give one specific example of how an honest historian uses primary and secondary sources together to tell a complete, fair story."
Scoring Guidance by Level
Secure — 3 pts
Explains honesty specifically as checking multiple sources AND reporting what the evidence actually shows — not just what the historian wants it to show. Ties to a specific example from the lesson (e.g., the Valley Forge letter shows suffering, but an honest historian would also find sources showing the soldiers' motivations and the British perspective). Shows understanding that honesty in historical work requires something like courage — reporting inconvenient evidence, acknowledging what you don't know, and correcting earlier errors.
Developing — 2 pts
General statement about truth-telling or checking sources. Mentions honesty and history but the connection is loose — may say "an honest historian checks their facts" without explaining what that looks like in practice. May not give a specific example from the lesson.
Beginning — 1 pt
Restates what honesty means (e.g., "telling the truth") without connecting it to the historian's specific practice of working with sources. Little or no historical example. Does not address the specific work of primary and secondary sources.
