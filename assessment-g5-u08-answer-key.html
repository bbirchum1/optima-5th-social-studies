Part 2 Source Task — Answer Key
Declaration of Independence, 1776
"…Governments are instituted among Men, deriving their just powers from the consent of the governed."
A
What does "consent of the governed" mean? Explain it in your own words using evidence.
Suggested Answer

"Consent of the governed" means that government gets its power from the people it rules — the people agree to be governed, and a government that fails to protect people's rights can be removed or changed. The Declaration states this directly: governments derive their "just powers from the consent of the governed," meaning authority flows upward from the people, not downward from a king. This was a direct argument that King George III had lost legitimacy because colonists never consented to his taxation without representation.

B
Who was NOT treated equally in 1776? Name at least two groups and explain the contradiction.
Suggested Answer — Accept any two or more of these groups

Enslaved Africans — held in bondage with no legal rights, no freedom, no vote; the very men who wrote "all men are created equal" enslaved hundreds of thousands of people. This is the Declaration's most glaring contradiction.

Women — had no right to vote, very limited legal rights, could not own property independently in most colonies. Abigail Adams explicitly called this out in her "Remember the Ladies" letter.

Indigenous peoples — not considered citizens; the Declaration itself refers to them as "merciless Indian Savages," revealing deep prejudice.

Poor white men without property — in many colonies, only property-owning men could vote, so even "free" men without land were excluded.

Strong responses will note the gap between the Declaration's universal language and the social reality of 1776 — and explain why that gap matters historically.

C
Is the Declaration a primary or secondary source? Who is the audience, and how might that affect what it says?
Suggested Answer

The Declaration is a primary source — it was written by Thomas Jefferson and the Continental Congress at the time of the event (1776) as a direct product of the Revolution itself.

The audience was multiple: the British Crown, the world (especially potential allies like France), and colonists themselves. Because it was written to justify rebellion and gain international support, it presents the colonial case in the strongest, most universal possible terms — "all men are created equal," "unalienable Rights." A historian must ask: what is this document not saying? It does not mention enslaved people, women, or Indigenous peoples. It was a persuasive document, not a neutral record, so it must be read as evidence of what its authors wanted the world to believe — not as a complete picture of 1776 American society.

Part 3 Hasty Herald — Scoring Guidance
"The American Revolution was simple — colonists were tired of taxes, so they fought back."
Scoring Notes — Part 3
Secure (3 pts)
Names and explains at least two specific causes beyond taxes. Strong responses may include any of the following: the Proclamation of 1763 restricting westward expansion after the French & Indian War (colonists felt their sacrifices were ignored); lack of colonial representation in Parliament ("no taxation without representation" was about political power, not just money); Enlightenment ideas about natural rights (John Locke's influence on Jefferson — the idea that governments derive legitimacy from the people); quartering of British troops in colonists' homes; the Boston Massacre (1770) as a symbol of military oppression that radicalized public opinion. Student connects causes with specific evidence and makes a clear, complete claim.
Developing (2 pts)
Names two causes beyond taxes but does not fully explain them or connect them to specific historical evidence. May list events without explaining their significance.
Beginning (1 pt)
Only mentions taxes or restates the Hasty Herald claim without meaningful addition. May name one additional cause with no explanation.

Part 4 Virtue Reflection — Scoring Guidance
Virtue: Courage — Write about one specific person or group who showed courage during this period and explain what made their courage meaningful.
Scoring Notes — Part 4
Secure (3 pts)
Names a specific person or group, describes what they did, and explains what made their courage meaningful given their particular circumstances. Strong examples include:

Crispus Attucks — first person killed at the Boston Massacre; as a free Black man, his courage is especially significant because he was fighting (and dying) for a country that did not fully recognize his rights as a citizen.

Abigail Adams — pushed for women's rights and inclusion ("Remember the Ladies") within a movement that largely ignored women. Her courage was in speaking up within a cause that did not extend its ideals to her.

Enslaved people who fought (for either side) — faced death not just in battle but from enslavers if caught. Some fought for the British after Lord Dunmore's Proclamation promised freedom; others fought for the Patriots hoping independence would eventually extend to them. Both were taking enormous risks for freedom in a society that denied it to them.

Women on the home front — managed farms and businesses, organized boycotts of British goods (Daughters of Liberty), worked as spies and messengers, with none of the recognition given to male soldiers.

Student response connects courage to specific stakes and identity, not just to general bravery.
Developing (2 pts)
Names a specific person and provides a general description of their courage. Does not fully explain what made their courage particularly meaningful or significant relative to their identity and circumstances.
Beginning (1 pt)
Makes a general statement about courage during the Revolution without naming a specific person or providing a meaningful explanation of what made it significant.
